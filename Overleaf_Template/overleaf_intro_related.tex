\section{Introduction}

The central question in this work is not whether a large language model (LLM) can produce a plausible answer to a women's-health question, but whether it first understands what the patient is trying to communicate.
The same concern may be stated clinically (``I have irregular menstrual bleeding''), colloquially (``my period is all over the place''), or indirectly (``my monthly problem has changed'').
Although these expressions can point to the same underlying concern, they differ in explicitness, emotion, and cultural register.
Misreading that variation can lead a model to confuse the concern, understate urgency, offer false reassurance, or answer when it should first ask for missing information \cite{recker-etal-2025-large,devries-etal-2025-enhancing}.

Most women's-health LLM studies, however, evaluate the generated answer: its accuracy, readability, empathy, or user preference.
For example, MenstLLaMA evaluates domain adaptation for menstrual-health education, while recent expert-led women's-health evaluations probe response safety across clinical scenarios \cite{adhikary-etal-2025-menstrual,maurya-etal-2026-whbench}.
These studies are important, but they do not isolate whether a matched clinical intent survives changes in surface form.
Multilingual health QA adds a second concern: models can give inconsistent health information when the same question is posed in different languages \cite{schlicht-etal-2025-consistent}.
Work from LUHME similarly shows that LLMs may compress cross-cultural variation, that sociocultural context affects how meaning should be adapted, and that successful dialogue depends on establishing common ground \cite{mohammadi-etal-2025-large,freitas-cardoso-2025-nightmare,anikina-etal-2025-building}.
Together, these findings motivate a controlled evaluation of \emph{interpretation} rather than another medical answer-generation study.

We therefore present \textbf{HerHealthEval} (Figure~\ref{fig:overview}), an initial investigation of language understanding in multilingual women's-health communication.
The controlled evaluation suite spans menstruation, polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)/hormonal concerns, and fertility; it holds the underlying case fixed while varying canonical, clinical, layperson, indirect/culturally sensitive, ambiguous, and emotionally concerned expression.
Matched English cases are localized into Arabic and French under a protocol that requires native-speaker approval of semantic preservation, naturalness, and register before they enter cross-lingual evaluation or adaptation.
Each item has predefined targets for concern category, risk level, recommended action, and clarification need.
The evaluation lineage is frozen separately from the adaptation corpus, with exclusions based on both source provenance and paraphrase-family identity.

We organize the study around three research questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    %\item \textbf{RQ1 (register):} How reliably do models preserve concern category, risk calibration, and action when the same case shifts from clinical to lay, indirect, ambiguous, or emotionally concerned language?
    %\item \textbf{RQ2 (language):} Are misunderstanding, under-triage, and clarification failures consistent across English, Arabic, and French?
    %\item \textbf{RQ3 (adaptation):} Compared with general-purpose Qwen3.5-9B, how do English domain adaptation and subsequent Arabic/French adaptation affect robust interpretation, especially appropriate clarification on under-specified inputs?
     \item \textbf{RQ1:}
    How robust are LLM-based clinical reasoning systems to variation in linguistic register when interpreting semantically equivalent women’s health concerns?
    \item \textbf{RQ2:}
    Do multilingual LLMs exhibit systematic differences in interpretation, triage calibration, and clarification behavior across English, Arabic, and French?
    \item \textbf{RQ3:}
    What is the impact of domain-specific and multilingual adaptation on linguistic robustness, uncertainty handling, and clarification generation in women’s health symptom understanding?
    
\end{enumerate}

The contributions align with these questions: (1) a formulation of women's-health interaction as a controlled language-understanding problem across register; (2) a multilingual evaluation framework with matched language variants, a native-speaker validation gate, and leakage-separated adaptation data; and (3) a reproducible, failure-oriented protocol for comparing general-purpose and sequentially adapted models using misunderstanding, triage, clarification, safety, and consistency measures.
These contributions are deliberately preliminary: the study establishes an evaluation protocol and supports analysis of early failure patterns rather than claiming comprehensive coverage or clinical readiness.

The remainder of this paper reviews related work (\S\ref{sec:related}), describes the evaluation suite and experimental protocol (\S\ref{sec:methods}), presents results and error analysis (\S\ref{sec:results}), and discusses limitations and ethical considerations (\S\ref{sec:discussion}).

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We cluster the closest work into three lines: women's-health answer evaluation, multilingual and sociocultural language understanding, and evaluation design for ambiguity and safety.

\subsection{Women's-Health Answer Evaluation}

Women's-health LLM research primarily asks whether generated answers are accurate, readable, empathetic, or safe \citep{recker-etal-2025-large,devries-etal-2025-enhancing}.
\citet{adhikary-etal-2025-menstrual} introduce MenstLLaMA, the closest domain-adaptation system, by fine-tuning LLaMA-3-8B on menstrual-health question--answer data and evaluating educational response quality in English.
\citet{mughal-etal-2025-mai} develop Mai, domain-specific menstrual-health dialogue systems in English and Roman Urdu; they explicitly motivate adaptation through local language, stigma, religious norms, and sociocultural barriers, but evaluate generated dialogue through automatic metrics and user ratings rather than matched tests of interpretation.
For PCOS, \citet{devranoglu-etal-2024-chatgpt} evaluate ChatGPT on clinician-authored questions about PCOS-related infertility, and \citet{graca-etal-2026-assessing} conduct an international blinded survey of health-professional ratings of ChatGPT and evidence-based self-management responses.
For fertility, \citet{chervenak-etal-2023-promise} compare ChatGPT counseling answers with public-health and professional sources, while \citet{gely-etal-2026-large} compare LLM answers to infertility FAQs with specialist answers on quality, accuracy, and empathy.
\citet{maurya-etal-2026-whbench} broaden women's-health coverage through WHBench, using expert-crafted scenarios and a safety-aware rubric.
Together, these studies motivate domain-specific adaptation and careful response evaluation, but they generally treat the input question as fixed; they do not test whether the same concern is interpreted consistently when its register or language changes.

\subsection{Multilingual and Sociocultural Understanding}

\citet{jin-etal-2024-better} introduce XLingEval to assess correctness, consistency, and verifiability on naturalistic healthcare questions across English, Spanish, Chinese, and Hindi, finding substantial cross-language disparities.
Subsequent multilingual health QA work likewise reports that equivalent questions can elicit inconsistent information across languages \citep{schlicht-etal-2025-consistent}.
Foundational cross-cultural NLP work distinguishes linguistic diversity from cultural variation and identifies linguistic form, common ground, topic relevance, and communicative objectives as separate dimensions of culturally situated language use \citep{hershcovich-etal-2022-challenges,adilazuarda-etal-2024-towards}.
In reproductive health specifically, participatory work with underserved women shows that culturally appropriate responses require local context beyond literal translation \citep{deva-etal-2025-family}, while evaluation rubrics built around Western assumptions can underrate responses judged appropriate by trained annotators and public-health experts \citep{dey-etal-2025-beyond}.
The LUHME literature identifies related problems: models compress observed cultural variation \citep{mohammadi-etal-2025-large}, text adaptation can fail when sociocultural context is ignored \citep{freitas-cardoso-2025-nightmare}, and dialogue systems require mechanisms for establishing common ground \citep{anikina-etal-2025-building}.
Cross-register transfer work further motivates holding semantic content constant when measuring sensitivity to linguistic form \cite{nicholls-alperin-2025-cross}.
Our setting brings these concerns together in matched English, Arabic, and French women's-health descriptions rather than testing general medical knowledge alone.

\subsection{Gap and Evaluation Requirements}

The resulting gap is an evaluation framework that separates understanding the input from generating an appealing answer.
Clarification research treats detecting the need to ask, selecting an informative question, and responding after clarification as distinct capabilities; current language models continue to struggle with when clarification is warranted \citep{testoni-fernandez-2024-asking,zhang-choi-2025-clarify}.
Such a framework must (i) hold the underlying concern constant across register and language, (ii) score concern interpretation, risk/action, and clarification need separately, (iii) expose under-triage and unsafe advice rather than rely on aggregate accuracy, and (iv) prevent overlap between evaluation and adaptation families.
Our controlled suite is positioned around these requirements.
Native-speaker review targets semantic and register preservation in Arabic and French, while source- and paraphrase-family exclusions separate evaluation from training.
The blinded clinical-rating designs used in PCOS and fertility studies motivate independent review of response quality \citep{graca-etal-2026-assessing,gely-etal-2026-large}, but our primary understanding measures remain tied to predefined item labels.
Because LLM judges can show only moderate agreement and systematic rubric biases \citep{donati-etal-2025-large}, rubric-scored cultural sensitivity, helpfulness, clarity, and safety are reported separately from deterministic label metrics and should be calibrated against independently reviewed responses.
