\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The central question in this work is not whether a large language model (LLM)
can produce a plausible answer to a women's-health question, but whether it
first understands what the patient is trying to communicate.
The same concern may be expressed clinically (``I have irregular menstrual
bleeding''), colloquially (``my period is all over the place''), or indirectly
(``what happens to me each month has changed'').
Although these formulations communicate the same underlying concern, they
differ in explicitness, emotion, linguistic register, and sociocultural
framing.

Comparable variation occurs across languages.
For example, the same irregular-menstruation concern may be expressed
clinically in French as
\foreignlanguage{french}{« Quelles sont les étiologies courantes des saignements
menstruels irréguliers ? »}
(``What are the common causes of irregular menstrual bleeding?''),
or more indirectly as
\foreignlanguage{french}{« Qu'est-ce qui pourrait expliquer un cycle menstruel qui change
sans cesse et n'est pas stable ? »}
(``What might explain a menstrual cycle that keeps changing and is not
stable?'').
In Modern Standard Arabic, the corresponding clinical formulation is
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ما هي الأسباب الشائعة لنزيف الدورة الشهرية غير المنتظم؟}
(``What are the common causes of irregular menstrual bleeding?''),
whereas a more indirect formulation is
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ما الذي قد يكون وراء دورة الحيض التي تتغير باستمرار وليست مستقرة؟}
(``What might be behind a menstrual cycle that keeps changing and is not
stable?'').
These examples are drawn from the matched, validated variants in our
evaluation suite.

Misinterpreting such variation can cause a model to confuse the underlying
concern, understate its urgency, offer false reassurance, recommend an
inappropriate action, or respond directly when it should first request
missing information
\cite{recker-etal-2025-large,devries-etal-2025-enhancing}.
These failures are especially consequential in women's-health communication,
where stigma, uncertainty, limited health literacy, and culturally indirect
language may shape how a person describes a sensitive concern.

Most women's-health LLM studies instead evaluate the generated answer rather than whether the input was understood, and multilingual health QA work shows the same question can elicit inconsistent information across languages. Together, these gaps motivate a controlled evaluation of \emph{interpretation} rather than another answer-generation study; Section \ref{sec:related} reviews this literature in full.


This paper presents \textbf{HerHealthEval}, an initial investigation of language understanding in multilingual women's-health communication.
The evaluation suite spans menstruation, PCOS/hormonal concerns, and fertility, holding each underlying case fixed while varying canonical, clinical, layperson, indirect/culturally sensitive, ambiguous, and emotionally concerned expression.
Matched English cases are localized into Arabic and French under several native-speakers-approved protocol before entering cross-lingual evaluation or adaptation.
Each item has predefined targets for concern category, risk level, recommended action, and clarification need, and the evaluation lineage is kept separate from the adaptation corpus by source- and paraphrase-family exclusion.

We organize the study around three research questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{RQ1:}
    How robust are LLMs to variation in linguistic register when interpreting
    semantically equivalent women's-health concerns?
    \item \textbf{RQ2:}
    Do LLMs exhibit systematic differences in concern interpretation, triage
    calibration, recommended action, and clarification behavior across
    English, Arabic, and French?
    \item \textbf{RQ3:}
    How do English domain adaptation and joint English--Arabic--French
    adaptation affect linguistic robustness, uncertainty handling, and
    clarification behavior in women's-health communication?
\end{enumerate}

The contributions align with these questions: (1) women's-health interaction framed as a controlled language-understanding problem across register; (2) a multilingual evaluation protocol with matched language variants, a native-speaker validation gate, and leakage-separated adaptation data; and (3) a reproducible, failure-oriented comparison of general-purpose and sequentially adapted models on misunderstanding, triage, clarification, safety, and consistency.
These contributions are deliberately preliminary: an evaluation protocol and early failure patterns, not comprehensive coverage or clinical readiness.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We cluster the closest work into three lines: women's-health answer evaluation, multilingual and sociocultural language understanding, and evaluation design for ambiguity and safety.

\subsection{Women's-Health Answer Evaluation}

Women's-health LLM research primarily asks whether generated answers are accurate, readable, empathetic, or safe \citep{recker-etal-2025-large,devries-etal-2025-enhancing}.
\citet{adhikary-etal-2025-menstrual} introduce MenstLLaMA, one of the closest domain-adaptation systems, fine-tuning LLaMA-3-8B on menstrual-health QA data and evaluating educational response quality in English.
\citet{mughal-etal-2025-mai} develop Mai, a menstrual-health dialogue system in English and Roman Urdu motivated by stigma and sociocultural barriers, but evaluated through automatic metrics and user ratings rather than matched interpretation tests.
For PCOS, \citet{devranoglu-etal-2024-chatgpt} evaluate ChatGPT on clinician-authored questions and \citet{graca-etal-2026-assessing} run an international blinded survey of health-professional ratings; a systematic review of 80 PCOS AI studies finds the broader field still dominated by imaging and structured predictive modeling \citep{ghaderzadeh-etal-2025-artificial}, so conversational LLM evaluation is a small slice of that literature.
For fertility, \citet{chervenak-etal-2023-promise} compare ChatGPT counseling with public-health sources, and \citet{gely-etal-2026-large} compare LLM answers to infertility FAQs against specialist answers.
\citet{maurya-etal-2026-whbench} broaden coverage through WHBench, an expert-crafted, safety-aware rubric.
These studies motivate domain-specific adaptation and careful response evaluation but treat the input question as fixed; none tests whether the same concern is interpreted consistently as its register or language changes.

\subsection{Multilingual and Sociocultural Understanding}

\citet{jin-etal-2024-better} introduce XLingEval to assess correctness, consistency, and verifiability on healthcare questions across English, Spanish, Chinese, and Hindi, finding substantial cross-language disparities; subsequent work likewise reports inconsistent information across languages for equivalent questions \citep{schlicht-etal-2025-consistent}.
Foundational cross-cultural NLP work distinguishes linguistic diversity from cultural variation, identifying linguistic form, common ground, topic relevance, and communicative objectives as separate dimensions \citep{hershcovich-etal-2022-challenges,adilazuarda-etal-2024-towards}.
In reproductive health, participatory work with underserved women shows culturally appropriate responses require local context beyond literal translation \citep{deva-etal-2025-family}, while Western-centric rubrics can underrate responses trained annotators judge appropriate \citep{dey-etal-2025-beyond}.
LUHME work identifies related problems: models compress cultural variation \citep{mohammadi-etal-2025-large}, adaptation can fail when sociocultural context is ignored \citep{freitas-cardoso-2025-nightmare}, and dialogue systems need mechanisms for common ground \citep{anikina-etal-2025-building}.
Concept-level terminological precision, not surface fluency, matters most where domain mismatches can alter meaning, as in healthcare \citep{heinisch-2025-terminologists}.
Cross-register transfer work further motivates holding semantic content constant when measuring sensitivity to linguistic form \cite{nicholls-alperin-2025-cross}.
Our setting brings these concerns together in matched English, Arabic, and French women's-health descriptions rather than testing general medical knowledge alone.

\subsection{Gap and Evaluation Requirements}

The resulting gap is a framework that separates understanding the input from generating an appealing answer.
Clarification research treats detecting the need to ask, selecting an informative question, and responding after clarification as distinct capabilities that current models still struggle with \citep{testoni-fernandez-2024-asking,zhang-choi-2025-clarify}; structured, stateful dialogue that elicits missing information turn by turn is one practical direction \citep{demarco-etal-2024-converso}.
Such a framework must (i) hold the underlying concern constant across register and language, (ii) score interpretation, risk/action, and clarification need separately, (iii) expose under-triage and unsafe advice rather than rely on aggregate accuracy, and (iv) prevent overlap between evaluation and adaptation data.
Native-speaker review targets semantic and register preservation in Arabic and French, while source- and paraphrase-family exclusions separate evaluation from training.
The blinded clinical-rating designs used in PCOS and fertility studies motivate independent review of response quality \citep{graca-etal-2026-assessing,gely-etal-2026-large}, but our primary measures remain tied to predefined item labels.
