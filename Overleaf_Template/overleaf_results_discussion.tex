\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We report results on the English evaluation set ($n=540$) for Qwen3.5-9B: a zero-shot baseline (M2) and three QLoRA adaptations --- structured (JSON) targets (M3), the same with 4$\times$ oversampling of clarification examples (M3+O), and a joint English+French+Arabic adaptation (M3-ML) that follows the M3+O recipe. M3-ML is additionally scored on newly translated, native-validated French and Arabic evaluation sets ($n=540$ each, identical gold labels to English since translation preserves case identity), against French and Arabic zero-shot baselines scored on the same items (\S\ref{sec:fr-results}). Gold labels are skewed, so we foreground majority-class baselines alongside accuracy-style numbers and treat per-class recall, under-triage, and clarification recall as the honest headlines. The risk majority-class baseline is 0.644 and the clarification majority-class baseline is 0.833 in every language.

\subsection{Zero-shot baseline (M2)}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports the zero-shot baseline (M2), an instruction-tuned Qwen3.5-9B prompted with our structured triage schema. M2 parses at 100\% and reaches 0.667 risk accuracy, only marginally above the 0.644 majority baseline. The safety-critical weakness is \emph{under-triage}: of 174 items whose gold risk is \texttt{see-doctor}, M2 routes 71.8\% to \texttt{routine} (per-class recall 0.264, versus 0.897 for \texttt{routine}). M2 retains strong clarification-seeking, however: recall 0.856 on the 90 items requiring clarification, at 0.862 specificity. Category accuracy is 0.539, with fertility weakest (recall 0.467). The model gives a consistent risk label to only 64.4\% of case groups across their six phrasings, and a consistent category to just 15.6\% --- direct evidence that surface communication style, not clinical content, drives much of the interpretation variance (RQ1, \ref{sec:rq-answers}).

\subsection{Fine-tuning reduces under-triage but erases clarification-seeking}

We adapt Qwen3.5-9B with QLoRA on leakage-controlled data drawn from the same case pool as the evaluation set but disjoint from it, using targets wrapped in the exact structured schema the evaluation items score against.

Adaptation reliably trades one behavior for another. All three adapted models keep near-perfect parse compliance ($\geq$0.996) and reduce under-triage below or near the zero-shot baseline, while more than doubling cross-style category consistency (0.156 for M2, rising to 0.344/0.378/0.411 for M3/M3+O/M3-ML). The best English triage is M3+O, which recovers risk accuracy to zero-shot parity (0.665 vs.\ 0.667) and attains the lowest under-triage of any model (0.605). But clarification-seeking collapses: from 0.856 for the zero-shot baseline to 0.044 for M3, and to 0.000 once the clarification examples are oversampled --- oversampling improves triage yet does \emph{not} restore clarification. Every adapted model drives clarification recall to near zero while pushing specificity toward 1.0; the adapted models simply stop asking and always answer. By McNemar's test, M3 is significantly worse than M2 on risk correctness (p$=$0.03) but not on category (p$=$0.71) or clarification (p$=$0.30); M3+O and M3-ML are statistically indistinguishable from M2 on every field ($p>0.16$), with their gains concentrated in the rate metrics (under-triage, consistency) rather than paired per-item accuracy. The multilingual model M3-ML gives the highest cross-style category consistency of any English configuration (0.411) but does not improve English triage over the English-only M3+O --- expected, since its added French/Arabic data carries no English triage signal. Its cross-lingual payoff is examined next (\S\ref{sec:fr-results}) and, on French, is a regression rather than a benefit.

\subsection{French evaluation exposes a safety regression invisible in English}
\label{sec:fr-results}

Table~\ref{tab:fr} compares M2 and M3-ML on identical items scored in English and French. Aggregate accuracy shifts by similar, modest amounts in both languages (risk accuracy $-$0.012 English, $-$0.039 French; category accuracy $-$0.029 English, $-$0.049 French) --- the kind of difference that would look unremarkable if accuracy were the only metric reported. The safety-relevant metrics tell a different story. Under-triage is roughly flat in English (0.718$\to$0.711) but collapses in French: M3-MLfr routes 173 of 174 \texttt{see-doctor} items to \texttt{routine} (under-triage 0.632$\to$0.994), a near-total loss of the model's ability to flag care-warranting French input (Figure~\ref{fig:regression}a). Clarification recall collapses to 0.000 in \emph{both} languages, so that failure is language-independent, but the triage collapse is not: it is specific to French and would be invisible from the English results alone (Figure~\ref{fig:regression}b). By McNemar's test, M3-MLfr is significantly worse than M2fr on both category correctness (p$=$0.017) and risk correctness (p$=$0.045), whereas the same English comparison (M3-ML vs.\ M2) is not significant on either field (p$=$0.164, p$=$0.550; Table~\ref{tab:main}). Cross-style risk consistency appears to \emph{improve} in French (0.544$\to$0.978) even more than in English, but this is the same collapse viewed through the consistency metric: a model that predicts \texttt{routine} for nearly every case is trivially consistent across its six register variants. Taken together, French M3-ML is simultaneously the most internally consistent and the least safe configuration we evaluate. The Arabic evaluation reproduces this pattern exactly: M3-ML routes 537 of 540 Arabic items to \texttt{routine} (under-triage 0.632$\to$0.994 relative to the Arabic zero-shot baseline, clarification recall 0.011), while the Arabic baseline itself behaves like the English and French ones (under-triage 0.638, clarification recall 0.889). The collapse is therefore systematic across both non-English languages rather than a French-specific artifact, and it lowers genuine cross-lingual agreement: three-way English--French--Arabic risk agreement falls from 0.846 for M2 to 0.804 for M3-ML, and M3-ML's near-perfect French--Arabic agreement (0.991) is itself an artifact of the shared \texttt{routine} collapse. The effect is statistically robust: 95\% bootstrap confidence intervals for M3-ML under-triage are [0.981, 1.000] in French and [0.981, 1.000] in Arabic, disjoint from the baseline's [0.561, 0.704] and [0.567, 0.709], whereas the two models' English under-triage intervals overlap ([0.637, 0.774] vs.\ [0.651, 0.785]). Table~\ref{tab:qual} illustrates the failure on representative cases.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Two results stand out. General-purpose Qwen3.5-9B under-triages the majority of care-warranting cases and interprets the same case differently depending only on phrasing (RQ1, \ref{sec:rq-answers}). Adaptation is a double-edged tool: it reduces under-triage and stabilizes category interpretation, and with clarification oversampling it recovers risk accuracy to baseline parity --- but across every variant, including the multilingual one, it erases clarification-seeking almost entirely, and oversampling the clarification examples to nearly a third of the training mixture does not bring it back. This suggests clarification on ambiguous input is not a skill that answer-style supervised fine-tuning teaches by example; recovering it likely needs objectives that explicitly reward abstention or asking --- preference optimization, or a dedicated clarification-vs-answer decision head --- rather than more demonstrations. The practical lesson for safety-relevant domains: evaluate adaptation on the structured behaviors that matter at deployment, triage \emph{and} clarification, not answer quality alone, since a model can improve on one while silently losing another.

The French comparison sharpens this lesson further: consistency and safety can move in opposite directions, and a same-language evaluation would have missed it entirely. M3-ML's French risk consistency (0.978) is the highest of any configuration in this study, yet it coincides with the worst under-triage rate we observe (0.994) --- the model is consistent because it has stopped distinguishing cases, not because it interprets them more reliably. This is the practical case for RQ2: evaluating an adaptation only in its dominant training language can report success (stable, consistent, parses cleanly) while the same model is actively unsafe in another language the same deployment would serve.

\subsection{Preliminary answers to the research questions}
\label{sec:rq-answers}

We revisit the three research questions against the evidence available at this stage; all are answered only as far as current results allow.

\paragraph{RQ1 (register robustness).} \emph{Partially answered, English only.} Holding a case fixed and varying only its phrasing changes the predicted risk label for 35.6\% of cases and the predicted category for 84.4\% (cross-style consistency 0.644 and 0.156). Adaptation partly redistributes this: category consistency more than doubles (to 0.344--0.411 across the adapted models) while risk consistency falls (to 0.433--0.500), so robustness gains are not uniform across target types.

\paragraph{RQ2 (cross-lingual differences).} \emph{Answered across English, French, and Arabic.} M3-ML shows a language-specific safety failure that aggregate accuracy does not surface: under-triage of \texttt{see-doctor} cases is roughly unchanged in English (0.718$\to$0.711) but collapses in both French (0.632$\to$0.994) and Arabic (0.638$\to$0.994), while clarification-recall collapse (to near 0.000) is shared across all three languages (\S\ref{sec:fr-results}). Both non-English languages also show large apparent gains in cross-style consistency (0.978 French, 0.967 Arabic), which we read as evidence of label collapse rather than improved robustness. The pattern replicates identically in French and Arabic, indicating a systematic effect of the adaptation recipe rather than a language-specific artifact.

\paragraph{RQ3 (adaptation impact).} \emph{Answered.} Domain adaptation reduces under-triage (0.718 to 0.605 for the best English recipe) and improves cross-style category consistency, at some cost to risk consistency and with clarification recall collapsing to near zero. The joint English+French+Arabic adaptation (M3-ML) preserves English triage near the English-only recipe, but its cross-lingual payoff is negative in both languages we can measure: on French and Arabic alike, the same adaptation drives under-triage to 0.994 (\S\ref{sec:fr-results}), a safety regression rather than a benefit, and it lowers three-way cross-lingual risk agreement (0.846$\to$0.804) below the untuned baseline.

\section*{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Results reported here are on matched English, French, and Arabic evaluation sets under a single protocol. The non-English collapse we report for the multilingual model is traced to language-asymmetric silver labels in the adaptation data (English-keyed risk heuristic applied to translated training text, \S\ref{sec:fr-results}); it characterizes this specific adaptation recipe, not an inherent limit of the base model, which handles both French and Arabic competently. Gold labels are skewed toward \texttt{routine} risk and \texttt{no}-clarification, which is why we foreground majority baselines, per-class recall, and under-triage over raw accuracy. Risk and clarification targets are a disclosed heuristic rather than clinician adjudication, applied identically to the evaluation set and the adaptation data (\ref{sec:gold-labels}); the adaptation targets in particular are silver labels used only for training, never for scoring. The \texttt{unsafe\_response} flag is model-self-reported, not an independently validated safety measure. The Arabic adaptation data uses Modern Standard Arabic only. Since Qwen3.5-9B is itself a multilingual model with pretrained exposure to Arabic dialects, this is a gap in our controlled data coverage rather than evidence that the model cannot handle dialectal input --- but we have not directly measured dialectal performance, and extending evaluation to dialectal Arabic (e.g., Egyptian, Gulf, Levantine) is a natural direction for future work. This initial study covers three symptom categories, three languages, and a single 9B model family, so generalization to other conditions, cultures, care settings, and model scales is untested. Arabic and French adaptation text was produced by LLM machine translation and confirmed by native-speaker/professional review for meaning and register (\ref{sec:methods}); this validates translation quality, not the clinical accuracy of gold targets. Model outputs are evidence of linguistic understanding in a controlled research setting, not medical advice or proof of clinical-deployment readiness.
