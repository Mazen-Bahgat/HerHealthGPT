\begin{abstract}
Large language models are increasingly used for women's-health patient education, yet most evaluations target answer \emph{generation} rather than safe \emph{interpretation} of symptom language across registers and languages.
We present \textbf{HerHealthGPT-LU}, a multilingual benchmark for evaluating LLM understanding of women's-health symptom communication in English, Arabic, and French.
The benchmark comprises 540 style-controlled items spanning menstruation, PCOS/hormonal concerns, and fertility, with gold labels for condition inference, risk calibration, recommended action, and clarification need.
We separate a frozen evaluation benchmark from leakage-controlled adaptation data and propose metrics targeting misunderstanding, unsafe responses, and cross-lingual consistency.
We report baseline and fine-tuned model results together with a systematic error taxonomy for women's-health language understanding.
\end{abstract}

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

All results below are on the frozen English benchmark ($n=540$: 90 seeds
$\times$ 6 communication styles). Gold labels are skewed, so we report
majority-class baselines alongside accuracy-style numbers and treat per-class
recall, under-triage, and clarification recall as the honest headlines. The
risk majority-class baseline is 0.644 and the clarification majority-class
baseline is 0.833.

\subsection{Zero-shot baseline (M2)}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports the zero-shot baseline (M2), an instruction-tuned
Qwen3.5-9B prompted with our structured triage schema. M2 parses at 100\% and
reaches 0.667 risk accuracy, only marginally above the 0.644 majority baseline.
The safety-critical weakness is \emph{under-triage}: of 174 items whose gold
risk is \texttt{see-doctor}, M2 routes 71.8\% to \texttt{routine}
(per-class recall for \texttt{see-doctor} is only 0.264, versus 0.897 for
\texttt{routine}). M2 does, however, retain strong clarification-seeking:
recall of 0.856 on the 90 items that require clarification, at 0.862
specificity. Category accuracy is 0.539, with fertility weakest (recall 0.467).
The model gives a consistent risk label to only 64.4\% of seed groups across
their six phrasings, and a consistent category to just 15.6\%, direct evidence
that surface communication style, not clinical content, drives much of the
variance in interpretation.

\subsection{Fine-tuning is highly sensitive to target format}

We fine-tune Qwen3.5-9B with QLoRA on leakage-controlled adaptation data drawn
from the same seed pool as the benchmark but disjoint from it (verbatim-question
and shared-answer overlaps are removed before training). We compare two target
formats. M3$_\text{QA}$ trains on plain question$\rightarrow$answer text;
M3$_\text{JSON}$ trains on targets wrapped in the exact structured schema the
benchmark scores.

Target format dominates the outcome. Training on plain answers
(M3$_\text{QA}$) degrades every structured behavior: JSON parse compliance
falls to 0.865, under-triage \emph{worsens} to 0.800, and clarification-seeking
collapses from 0.856 to 0.174, because the plain-answer targets never model the
structured triage act the benchmark measures. Reformatting the identical
training content into the eval schema (M3$_\text{JSON}$) restores parse
compliance to 0.998, reduces under-triage below the zero-shot baseline to 0.647,
and more than doubles cross-style category consistency (0.156 to 0.344). By
McNemar's paired test on the 540 items, M3$_\text{JSON}$ differs from M2
significantly on risk (p$=$0.03, favoring M2) but not on category (p$=$0.71) or
clarification (p$=$0.30) correctness; parse compliance is statistically
unchanged (p$=$1.0).

A cost remains: even in the correct format, fine-tuning erodes
clarification-seeking (recall 0.044). The adaptation distribution is dominated
by direct-answer targets, which overwrite the base model's willingness to ask
for clarification on vague input. This isolates the tension cleanly: fine-tuning
buys triage safety and cross-style consistency but, at this data mixture, at the
expense of clarification behavior. We are evaluating an oversampling remedy that
rebalances the clarification signal in the training mixture; those numbers are
pending and will be added on completion.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Two findings stand out. First, women's-health symptom \emph{understanding} is
not solved by strong general models: our zero-shot baseline under-triages the
majority of care-warranting cases and interprets the same clinical situation
differently depending only on how a patient phrases it. The benchmark's
style-controlled design makes this measurable, since each seed appears in six
registers with a single gold interpretation.

Second, adaptation is a double-edged tool. Fine-tuning can reduce under-triage
and stabilize interpretation across styles, but it is acutely sensitive to how
training targets are formatted and mixed: a naive question--answer objective
silently destroys both output-format compliance and the model's clarification
behavior. For a safety-relevant domain, this argues for evaluating adaptation
on the same structured behaviors one cares about at deployment, rather than on
answer quality alone, and for explicitly budgeting the training mixture for
behaviors, such as asking for clarification, that direct-answer data suppresses.

\section*{Limitations}

The results reported here are on the English benchmark only; the Arabic and
French arms of the benchmark, and the multilingual fine-tune, are in progress
and not yet reported. Gold labels are skewed toward \texttt{routine} risk and
\texttt{no}-clarification, which is why we foreground majority baselines,
per-class recall, and under-triage rather than raw accuracy. Silver risk and
clarification labels used for the adaptation data are derived heuristically from
answer text and are not clinician-adjudicated; they are used only for training,
never for benchmark scoring. The benchmark covers three women's-health areas
(menstruation, PCOS/hormonal, fertility) and a single 9B model family, so
generalization to other conditions and model scales is untested. The
\texttt{unsafe\_response} flag is model-self-reported and is not an independently
validated safety measure. Finally, this work evaluates language understanding
and triage calibration; it is not a clinical decision tool and must not be used
for diagnosis or treatment.
