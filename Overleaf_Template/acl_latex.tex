\begin{abstract}
Large language models are increasingly used to answer women's-health questions, but fluent answers do not establish that a model has correctly understood what a patient is trying to communicate.
We present HerHealthEval, a multilingual benchmark and evaluation framework for studying language understanding in women’s-health communication across languages and linguistic registers.

Our controlled evaluation suite covers menstruation, polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)/hormonal concerns, and fertility in English, Arabic, and French, pairing clinical, lay, indirect, ambiguous, and emotionally concerned expressions with predefined targets for concern category, risk level, recommended action, and clarification need.
The protocol requires native-speaker validation of Arabic and French for meaning preservation, naturalness, and register before cross-lingual evaluation or adaptation, while evaluation cases remain separated from leakage-controlled adaptation data.
The experimental design centers Qwen3.5-9B and specifies a general-purpose baseline, English domain adaptation, and subsequent Arabic/French adaptation, evaluated through misunderstanding, under-triage, clarification, unsafe-response, and cross-style/cross-language consistency measures.
Rather than claiming comprehensive clinical coverage, this preliminary study establishes a failure-oriented protocol for testing women's-health language understanding beyond answer generation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We report two complementary evaluations. Section~\ref{sec:results-en-pilot} is
an English-only pilot that isolates how supervised fine-tuning reshapes
structured triage behavior (target-format sensitivity and clarification
collapse). Section~\ref{sec:results-multilingual} is the main multilingual
evaluation on the aligned HerHealthEval benchmark (English, French, Arabic on
the same seeds), which measures cross-language consistency and cross-lingual
under-triage. Gold labels are skewed toward \texttt{see-doctor}, so throughout
we report majority-class baselines alongside accuracy and treat per-class
recall, under-triage, and clarification recall as the honest headlines.

\subsection{English pilot: fine-tuning reshapes triage behavior}
\label{sec:results-en-pilot}

The pilot uses a frozen English benchmark ($n=540$: 90 seeds $\times$ 6
communication styles; risk majority baseline 0.644, clarification majority
baseline 0.833).

\subsubsection{Zero-shot baseline (M2)}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports the zero-shot baseline (M2), an instruction-tuned
Qwen3.5-9B prompted with our structured triage schema. M2 parses at 100\% and
reaches 0.667 risk accuracy, only marginally above the 0.644 majority baseline.
The safety-critical weakness is \emph{under-triage}: of 174 items whose gold
risk is \texttt{see-doctor}, M2 routes 71.8\% to \texttt{routine}
(per-class recall for \texttt{see-doctor} is only 0.264, versus 0.897 for
\texttt{routine}). M2 does, however, retain strong clarification-seeking:
recall of 0.856 on the 90 items that require clarification, at 0.862
specificity. Category accuracy is 0.539, with fertility weakest (recall 0.467).
The model gives a consistent risk label to only 64.4\% of seed groups across
their six phrasings, and a consistent category to just 15.6\%, direct evidence
that surface communication style, not clinical content, drives much of the
variance in interpretation.

\subsubsection{Fine-tuning is highly sensitive to target format}

We fine-tune Qwen3.5-9B with QLoRA on leakage-controlled adaptation data drawn
from the same seed pool as the benchmark but disjoint from it (verbatim-question
and shared-answer overlaps are removed before training). We compare two target
formats. M3$_\text{QA}$ trains on plain question$\rightarrow$answer text;
M3$_\text{JSON}$ trains on targets wrapped in the exact structured schema the
benchmark scores.

Target format dominates the outcome. Training on plain answers
(M3$_\text{QA}$) degrades every structured behavior: JSON parse compliance
falls to 0.865, under-triage \emph{worsens} to 0.800, and clarification-seeking
collapses from 0.856 to 0.174, because the plain-answer targets never model the
structured triage act the benchmark measures. Reformatting the identical
training content into the eval schema (M3$_\text{JSON}$) restores parse
compliance to 0.998, reduces under-triage below the zero-shot baseline to 0.647,
and more than doubles cross-style category consistency (0.156 to 0.344). By
McNemar's paired test on the 540 items, M3$_\text{JSON}$ differs from M2
significantly on risk (p$=$0.03, favoring M2) but not on category (p$=$0.71) or
clarification (p$=$0.30) correctness; parse compliance is statistically
unchanged (p$=$1.0).

\subsubsection{Clarification-seeking does not survive fine-tuning}

Adding structured targets recovers format but not clarification: M3$_\text{J}$
asks for clarification on almost none of the 90 items that require it (recall
0.044). We tested whether this is a simple data-balance problem by
oversampling the ambiguous (clarification) training rows four-fold, raising them
from 10.5\% to 32\% of the mixture (M3$_\text{J+O}$). Oversampling further
improves triage---M3$_\text{J+O}$ matches the zero-shot baseline on risk
accuracy (0.665 vs.\ 0.667), attains the lowest under-triage of any model
(0.605), and the highest cross-style category consistency (0.378)---but
clarification recall does not recover; it falls to 0.000. Every fine-tuned
variant, regardless of format or clarification-oversampling, drives
clarification-seeking to near zero while pushing specificity toward 1.0: the
adapted models simply stop asking and always answer. Clarification-seeking on
genuinely ambiguous input thus behaves as a fragile, emergent instruction-following
capability of the base model that supervised fine-tuning on answer-style data
robustly erases, and that naive rebalancing of the training mixture does not
restore. By McNemar's test, M3$_\text{J+O}$ is statistically indistinguishable
from M2 on risk (p$=$0.92), category (p$=$0.70), and clarification (p$=$0.20)
correctness, and on parse compliance (p$=$0.48); its gains are concentrated in
the safety-oriented rate metrics (under-triage, over-triage) and cross-style
consistency rather than in raw accuracy.

\subsection{Multilingual evaluation on HerHealthEval}
\label{sec:results-multilingual}

We now evaluate on the aligned multilingual benchmark
(\texttt{benchmark\_multilingual\_v1}): the same 90 \texttt{menst-*} seeds
rendered in English, French, and Arabic across the six styles
($n=540$ per language, $1{,}620$ parallel items), with identical gold labels
across languages. Because every seed appears in all three languages, this set
supports \emph{cross-language consistency} and \emph{meaning-preservation}
measurement, which the English pilot cannot. Category is balanced
(180/180/180; majority baseline 0.333); risk is uniformly \texttt{see-doctor}
(under-triage is the honest headline); 24/540 items require clarification.

We compare the base multilingual model (M2, Qwen3.5-9B zero-shot) against the
fine-tuned multilingual model. To isolate the effect of \emph{label quality} we
report two fine-tunes: \mlvone{}, whose non-English risk labels were corrupted
by an English-only risk heuristic applied to translated answers (mislabeling
$99.8\%$ of French/Arabic training rows \texttt{routine}), and \mlvtwo{}, which
derives risk from the English source by \texttt{row\_id} so labels are
language-independent.

\subsubsection{Cross-lingual under-triage from language-dependent labels}

Our clearest ``why models misunderstand non-English'' result is a controlled
ablation. \mlvone{} \emph{understands} French: on the French benchmark it parses
at 0.998 and reaches category accuracy 0.605 (higher than its English
category accuracy), yet it routes $539/540$ French items to \texttt{routine}
(under-triage $\approx 1.0$) --- it almost never recommends seeing a doctor in
French. The cause is not comprehension but supervision: the fine-tuning
pipeline derived risk labels by matching \emph{English} consult words against
the \emph{translated} answers, so $99.8\%$ of French (and Arabic) training rows
were labeled \texttt{routine}. Recovering risk from the English source by
\texttt{row\_id} (\mlvtwo{}) restores the training risk distribution to match
English (routine $0.58$ / see-doctor $0.40$). This demonstrates that
cross-lingual safety failures can arise from \emph{language-dependent label
quality} rather than from the model's inability to understand the non-English
input --- a failure mode invisible to English-only evaluation.

\section*{Limitations}

This initial study covers three symptom categories and three languages and therefore does not represent the full diversity of women's-health concerns, dialects, cultures, or care settings. Native-speaker validation assesses translation quality and preservation of register; it is not a substitute for clinical validation of every gold target. The current risk and clarification labels are clinically motivated but require further expert review, and their class imbalance limits conclusions drawn from aggregate accuracy. Finally, model outputs are evaluated as evidence of linguistic understanding in a controlled research setting, not as medical advice or proof of readiness for clinical deployment.
